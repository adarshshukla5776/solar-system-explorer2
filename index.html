Planet

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in the Solar System, orbiting the Sun every 88 Earth days. Its heavily cratered surface preserves billions of years of cosmic history, while extreme temperatures ranging from scorching heat to freezing cold make it one of the most hostile worlds known.

Planet

Venus

Venus, often called Earth's twin due to its similar size and structure, is the hottest planet in the Solar System. A dense atmosphere composed mainly of carbon dioxide traps heat through an extreme greenhouse effect, creating surface temperatures exceeding 460°C.

Saturn is renowned for its breathtaking ring system, composed of countless particles of ice and rock orbiting the planet at incredible speeds. As the second-largest planet, Saturn hosts a diverse family of moons, including Titan, a world with rivers, lakes, and a dense atmosphere that resembles conditions on early Earth.

Planet

Uranus

Uranus is an ice giant unlike any other planet, rotating on its side at an angle of nearly 98 degrees. This unusual orientation produces some of the most extreme seasonal patterns in the Solar System. Beneath its blue-green atmosphere lies a cold and mysterious interior rich in water, ammonia, and methane ices.

Planet

Neptune

Neptune is the farthest major planet from the Sun and is known for its powerful supersonic winds. Its deep blue color comes from methane in its atmosphere, and it remains one of the coldest worlds in the Solar System.

Dwarf planet

Pluto

Once recognized as the ninth planet, Pluto is now classified as a dwarf planet residing in the Kuiper Belt. Far from being a frozen relic, Pluto features towering ice mountains, vast nitrogen glaciers, and a surprisingly complex atmosphere. Its exploration by NASA's New Horizons mission revealed a world far more active and fascinating than previously imagined.
